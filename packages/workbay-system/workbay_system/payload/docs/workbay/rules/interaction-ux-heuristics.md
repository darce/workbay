# Interaction & UX Heuristics Lexicon

## About this document

> **Referenced, not read end-to-end.** A decision rubric beside design-aesthetics, engineering, accessibility, and ml-systems. It governs the **perception, cognition, behaviour, and correction of the human surface** — how users group, miss, expect, scan, remember, choose, navigate, act, and correct. It does **not** own visual tokens, contrast, or spacing (engineering `UI-*`) or graph partition (`GRPH-*`); it owns the **meaning** of spacing, similarity, placement, action, and error relative to human vision and memory.

**Division of responsibility** (per the expansion brief): design-aesthetics chooses visual direction; **interaction-ux governs perception, behaviour, and correction**; engineering `UI-*` implements the interface correctly; accessibility constrains every layer; ml-systems governs model evidence, uncertainty, and operations.

**What it covers.** Eight populated families. **PERC** (Gestalt grouping, perception, attention, figure/ground) · **COG** (memory, cognitive load, recognition-vs-recall, decision cost) · **NAV** (information architecture, wayfinding, information scent) · **INT** (feedback, direct manipulation, undo, modes, state transitions) · **FORM** (data entry, validation, recovery, destructive actions) · **HAI** (human-AI interaction, calibrated reliance, correction) · **VIZ** (data-to-channel encoding: quantity/magnitude channels, glyph design, colour-for-data and colormaps, layering, comparison and coordinated views, node-link grammar) · **UXR** (usability research and measurement: task success, time-on-task, errors, self-report, issue severity, participant count, and validation level). VIZ owns *how evidence is drawn*; it does **not** own uncertainty display — that stays with `HAI-08` (uncertainty at the decision granularity). New rows enter at the lowest defensible tier and are promoted on independent convergence.

> **Provenance — primary-source, chapter-traced.** Every row cites a primary source distilled to `distilled/interaction/` (or `distilled/ml-systems/` for the HITL-adjacent Monarch source): Johnson, *Designing with the Mind in Mind*, 3rd ed. (Morgan Kaufmann 2020); Tidwell, Brewer & Valencia, *Designing Interfaces*, 3rd ed. (O'Reilly 2019); Shneiderman, *Human-Centered AI* (Oxford 2022); Monarch, *Human-in-the-Loop Machine Learning* (Manning 2021); Ware, *Information Visualization: Perception for Design*, 4th ed. (Morgan Kaufmann 2020) and Munzner, *Visualization Analysis and Design* (CRC Press 2014) for **VIZ**; Albert & Tullis, *Measuring the User Experience*, 3rd ed. (Morgan Kaufmann 2022) for **UXR** and Macfadyen, *Designing AI Interfaces* (O'Reilly 2026) for the `HAI-12`–`HAI-14` and `INT-11` human-AI rules — the last a partial (ch-4/ch-5 only) source pending re-distillation and corroboration. The `Src` column names the distillation slug; the mechanism, exemptions, and worked failures live there.

**Scope boundary.** Decision heuristics for the human surface, not an HCI textbook: *what the interface must do so a person groups, notices, remembers, acts, and corrects correctly*, not the psychology proof beneath it. The mechanism stays in the source.

Row format matches the sibling lexicons: `ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src` — tier **B**locker (a user is systematically misled, misses a critical state, commits an unintended action, or loses data) / **S**hould (strong default) / **J**udgment; phase **p**lan (IA / interaction design) / **w**rite (implementation) / **r**eview (screenshot / walkthrough). IDs (`[PERC-01]`) are stable citation keys, immutable once assigned. Cross-lexicon borders cite `↔ ui/a11y/grph [X]` rather than restating them.

## 1. PERC — Perception, Gestalt grouping, attention

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PERC-01 | The gap between a label and its control is ≥ the gap to a neighbour, or a one-item group box | **Proximity binds meaning** — space-encode groups so each label sits nearer its target than any distractor; the eye reads the wrong association from the spacing alone (Gestalt proximity) (↔ eng [UI-03], design [LAY-03] all three read grouping from spacing alone, so the gap is the claim) | Is each label closer to its control than to any other? | S·r | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| PERC-02 | Identical visual style on unrelated items, or a critical status lacks a distinctive feature | **Similarity encodes semantics** — a shared look promises shared meaning, so unify the look only for shared behaviour and give status a distinctive feature, or a similarity pass invents false groups (Gestalt similarity) | Would a Gestalt similarity pass invent a wrong group? | S·r | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| PERC-03 | A finished layout claims a multi-element grouping | **Seven-principle Gestalt audit** — before shipping a grouped display, check proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, symmetry, figure-ground, and common-fate for relations you did not intend (↔ grph [GRPH-22], eng [DOM-03]; ↔ [HAI-05] a perceived autonomy boundary the system does not hold; ↔ [SEC-01] trust changes at every crossing so an unenforced boundary is only in the mind; ↔ [CLM-03] a policy promise against a divergent implementation; ↔ [WEB-08] route check without object auth is a boundary the eye trusts and the server does not) | Does any Gestalt principle invent a relation we don't mean? | S·r | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| PERC-04 | An ambiguous figure, icon, or chart admits two or more parses that differ by task or prime | **Disambiguate or prime** — resolve a multi-parse display with convention, a legend, or a prime, and test that two differently-primed cohorts parse it the same; otherwise the same pixels drive opposite actions (↔ ml [MLDATA-03] resolve label multiplicity in writing before "gold" is claimed) | Do two primed cohorts parse this display the same way? | B·r | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| PERC-05 | New critical information or an error appears away from the fovea after an action | **Fovea-first feedback** — place post-action status at or near the user's fovea with a distinctive mark; the periphery is low-resolution, so the change is missed and the user re-submits into a blank (↔ a11y non-colour status) | Where is the user's fovea at the moment this appears? | B·w | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| PERC-06 | A target must be found among many distractors that differ only by fine shape or detail | **Make it pop** — encode the target with weight, colour, motion, or gross shape the periphery can detect, or visual search stays linear in the number of items (↔ eng [UI-02], a11y non-colour signalling) | Does the target pop without a foveal scan? | S·w | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| PERC-07 | A strong interrupt (modal, blink, beep, confirm) fires on a high-frequency path | **Don't habituate alarms** — reserve strong attention-getting methods for rare, critical, irreversible events and suppress the routine, or users learn to dismiss the one that matters | Is this interrupt rarer and worse than the routine noise around it? | B·p | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |

## 2. COG — Memory, cognitive load, decision cost

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| COG-01 | A multi-step or voice flow requires holding more than ~4 novel, unrelated items off-screen | **Externalize working memory** — show the residue and current state, split long voice turns, and keep spoken option lists to ≤4–5; span beyond ~3–5 chunks drops the goal or a prior answer | What must the user hold that the UI does not show? | S·p | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| COG-02 | A frequent action requires a typed command or buried recall with no chooser | **Recognition over recall** — let a broad audience see-or-hear-and-choose rather than recall-and-type; pure recall means non-discovery (↔ security — an authentication scheme that overloads recall gets written down or worked around) | Can a new user choose without remembering a string? | S·w | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| COG-03 | The success path depends on the user noticing non-goal promo or chrome | **Design to the goal filter** — put goal-scented actions on the path and never require noticing orthogonal content; perception is filtered by the user's current goal, so off-goal cues are systematically missed | Does every required cue map to words of the user's goal? | B·p | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| COG-04 | A high-stakes choice is worded as only gains or only losses, or forces mental calculation | **Support System 2** — present a neutral or dual frame side by side and compute the comparison for the user; a one-sided frame reverses the preference the user would reflectively hold (↔ [Principle 13]; ↔ [GTM-01] only a costly action is demand evidence before inventory commit; ↔ [TEST-03] pin actual behaviour before the edit commits a new contract; ↔ [AIPX-01] ship the heuristic baseline before the ML commitment) | Can the deliberate mind compare options without re-wording tricks? | B·p | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |

## 3. NAV — Information architecture, wayfinding, information scent

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| NAV-01 | Hierarchy depth exceeds two levels for a general audience, with no persistent path | **Broad-shallow with a location path** — prefer breadth over depth and show a persistent "you are here" breadcrumb; depth loses the user's place and goal in working memory | Can the user answer "where am I and how deep?" from the chrome alone? | S·p | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| NAV-02 | A menu, toolbar, or list silently reorders, or a function moves between steps | **Stable positions** — keep learnable items in the same place and the same function in the same place across steps; re-cue any move loudly, or every use becomes a fresh linear search (↔ eng [NAME-04] consistency beats local quality) | Does item order or control position change without the user asking? | S·w | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| NAV-03 | A prominent visual cue (hero, large colour field) changes on the same route with no section signature | **Stable place cues** — keep a stable per-section visual signature and don't thrash prominent "where" cues without navigation, or a large visual change reads as a new page | Would a large visual change be mistaken for a page change? | S·r | designing-with-the-mind-in-mind |
| NAV-04 | Visual design is signed off before a site map or screen-type inventory exists | **IA before presentation** — freeze visual lock-in until the structure, labels, nav model, and screen types exist, or a pretty UI hard-codes a taxonomy that blocks finding and doing (↔ design [LAY-08] content-out before moodboard; ↔ biz [PROD-02] problem before solution) | Is there a site map and screen-type list before visual sign-off? | S·p | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-05 | Top-level nav labels have overlapping homes or a live task with no home | **MECE categories** — make primary categories mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive; a dual-home or a gap means the user cannot choose where to look or file (↔ grph [GRPH-22]; ↔ [CLM-03] policy text and implementation must share one home; ↔ [TEAM-01] an architectural split the org does not communicate across is a boundary only on the diagram) | Can every in-scope item name exactly one primary nav home? | S·p | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-06 | A high-frequency task is buried as deep as rare settings | **Frequency elevates chrome** — put frequent actions in global/top chrome and bury rare ones behind disclosure, or daily work pays a permanent search tax | Are the top tasks reachable without a submenu drill? | S·p | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-07 | A wizard, modal, 404, or deep-link landing offers no path back to full navigation | **Escape Hatch required** — from any limited-nav or out-of-context screen, offer one labelled return to a known place, or the user is trapped and abandons | From this screen, is there one obvious out to a known place? | B·w | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-08 | A first-run or home screen has no dominant task actions amid dense chrome | **Clear Entry Points** — present a few plain-language front-door actions and demote secondary links, or new and infrequent users hit "now what?" paralysis (↔ [RLSE-04] empty/first-time are designed states not blanks; ↔ [CAL-02] unknown is a valid result — a void is not a forced guess; ↔ [STRAT-03] too-tough is a designed basket not fake fluency) | Do new users see a few obvious start doors? | S·r | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-09 | A linear wizard, checkout, or onboarding has no step map | **Progress Indicator on linear flows** — show the ordered, named steps with current and remaining, or users cannot estimate the finish or re-enter a prior step | Does each step show how far is left and what the steps are named? | S·w | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-10 | A large catalog or content product offers only search or only browse on entry | **Feature, Search, and Browse** — a large item set needs all three entry modes, or known-item and exploratory users each lose half their find strategies | Can both a known-item and an exploratory user start without help? | S·p | designing-interfaces-nav |
| NAV-11 | A shareable or restorable view (map, filter, doc) does not round-trip through the URL | **Deep Links encode place and state** — put location and critical view params in the URL (excluding preference-stomping state), or the view cannot be shared or restored | Does opening the URL restore the same place and view? | S·w | designing-interfaces-nav |

## 4. INT — Feedback, direct manipulation, undo, state transitions

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| INT-01 | An interactive control lacks an action cue, or a non-interactive element carries button/link styling | **Affordance match** — actionable things show action cues (shape / contrast / cursor / role) and non-actionable things drop them, or users miss the control or false-click a decoration (↔ eng [NAME-03] a name that promises behaviour the code does not hold) | Does every clickable look clickable, and every non-clickable not? | B·r | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-02 | A `<select>` or dropdown triggers a side-effecting command on change | **Dropdowns select, buttons act** — a dropdown only changes a value; put verbs on buttons or menus, or a stray change commits an unintended action (↔ a11y [A11Y-20] no surprise context change on input) | Does changing this dropdown only change a value? | S·r | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-03 | Buttons of different scope are co-clustered, or same-scope actions are scattered | **Button group by scope** — cluster 2–5 same-scope actions with a shared treatment and separate different scopes; Gestalt proximity must match action scope (↔ [PERC-01] Gestalt proximity must match the action's scope, or the eye reads the wrong cluster) | Are app-wide and item-specific actions visually separated? | S·r | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-04 | Per-item actions are hover-only, with no touch or keyboard path, or reflow on hover | **Item tools reach every input** — progressive-disclose per-item tools but give mouse, touch, and keyboard each a path, with no hover reflow (↔ a11y [A11Y-11] keyboard walk; ↔ a11y [A11Y-15] single-pointer alternative) | Can mouse, touch, and keyboard all reach the item actions? | S·w | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-05 | The final commit is a link, low-contrast, far from the last field, or a peer of secondaries | **Prominent Done** — the final commit is one unmistakable button with a verb label at the end of the flow near the last control, or users abandon or pick the wrong next step | Is there one unmistakable final action after the last input? | S·r | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-06 | An Undo / Delete / Add label omits its target, or is enabled with an empty selection | **Smart action labels** — the label names the exact object and operation and disables when there is none, or the user acts on the wrong object (↔ a11y [A11Y-04] name states destination/action) | Does the label say exactly what will be affected? | B·w | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-07 | A heavyweight, costly, or hard-to-predict action commits with no outcome sample | **Preview before commit** — when cost or unpredictability is high, show the outcome with commit and back-out on the same surface (↔ [Principle 13]; ↔ [COG-04] compute the comparison for the deliberate mind before a high-stakes choice; ↔ [CLM-05] the hostile reader must check the claim before it ships; ↔ [STRAT-02] name the disconfirmers before the forward plan) | Can the user see the result before they pay / print / apply? | B·p | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-08 | An operation runs longer than ~1s with no progress, or a long job has no cancel | **Wait state and cancel** — over ~1s show progress (a meter if the duration is known); over ~2s and interruptible, offer a side-effect-free Cancel, or users double-submit or abandon (↔ eng [API-07] 202 + poll/cancel resource; ↔ a11y [A11Y-21] announce status) | If this takes over 1s, are progress and cancel obvious? | B·w | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-09 | An editor mutates data with no multilevel undo stack | **Reversible command stack** — model discrete reversible operations with a LIFO undo deep enough to reverse an exploration (excluding pure navigation), or edits cannot be safely undone (↔ [Principle 3]; ↔ [HAI-02] a correction that does not reach the source is undone by the next recompute; ↔ [DATA-14] patch the single authority not a divergent copy; ↔ [HITL-01] close the loop so the next train run consumes the correction; ↔ [AIPX-03] production use is the label source the next model reads; ↔ [SERVE-04] a post-hoc corrector on a frozen model never updates the source of error) | Can the user undo the last N permanent-ish edits? | B·p | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| INT-10 | AI or automation mutates content or state with no status, predictable next step, or stop | **Status–predict–stop** — automated action exposes its status, the predictable next step, and a cancel/undo; prefer an optional suggestion over a silent auto-commit, or the user cannot intervene (↔ [HAI-04] for AI effectors) | Can the user see state, predict the next action, and stop it? | B·w | human-centered-ai |
| INT-11 | recovery from a bad or partial generation is full-restart-only (delete and re-prompt), discarding valid work | **Refine over restart** — offer regenerate-in-place, edit-and-retry, and section-level rewrite before a full reset, and on a soft or partial failure preserve the good prefix instead of wiping it (↔ [INT-09] undo stack, [HAI-12] output is a proposal) | Does recovery preserve the part that already works? | S·w | designing-ai-interfaces |

## 5. FORM — Data entry, validation, recovery, destructive actions

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| FORM-01 | A form asks derivable fields, has no required/optional convention, or uses placeholder-only labels | **Minimize fields and mark requirement** — drop or derive redundant inputs, use one required/optional scheme, and keep visible labels, or users abandon or error | Which fields are required, and why is each one asked? | S·p | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| FORM-02 | Locale-variable text is forced into a rigid mask, or a fixed code sits in one free field | **Forgiving vs structured format** — parse and normalize variable formats, and segment fixed short codes with auto-advance, or valid international input is falsely rejected | Is this field's format fixed globally or user-variable? | S·w | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| FORM-03 | A non-obvious field has no example, or the placeholder is the only label | **Hints without fake-filled fields** — give an accessible label plus a short external hint or example and never rely on a placeholder alone (it vanishes on focus) (↔ a11y [A11Y-03] placeholder is not a label) | Will a first-time user know what to type without guessing? | S·r | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| FORM-04 | An empty form ignores known context, or pre-checks marketing / sensitive defaults | **Smart prefills, not dark defaults** — prefill high-confidence low-sensitivity fields but never default a password, sensitive identity, or an ad/consent opt-in (↔ sec consent; [LAY-10] unchosen defaults) | What is prefilled, and is any of it consent-sensitive? | B·r | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| FORM-05 | A validation error shows in a modal, a separate page, or a toast, with no field link, or only after full submit | **Inline actionable errors** — show the error at its field — which, why, how to fix — as soon as it is knowable, and keep it visible while editing, or recovery fails (↔ a11y [A11Y-17] errors name the field and the fix) | Can the user fix the error without memorizing a vanished message? | B·w | designing-interfaces-int-form |
| FORM-06 | High-volume discrete input is mouse-only or uses non-native widgets | **Keyboard-complete, native controls** — make discrete entry keyboard-completable and use native semantic controls for the input type, or throughput and quality drop (↔ a11y [A11Y-11] keyboard walk; ↔ a11y [A11Y-12] native first) | Can a user complete this without the mouse, on native controls? | S·w | human-in-the-loop-ml |

## 6. HAI — Human-AI interaction, calibrated reliance, correction

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| HAI-01 | An identity, label, or action appears without the source observations that produced it | **Evidence before label** — every machine claim links to the frames, timestamps, tracks, and model that produced it, and the reviewer can inspect that evidence in one action, or reliance is uncalibrated (↔ ml-systems [PROV-01], [Principle 9] both fail when a label ships without the evidence that produced it) | Can the reviewer inspect decisive evidence in one action? | B·r | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-02 | A human correction edits the display text but not the cluster, track, or model input | **Correction must reach the source** — a correction updates the underlying model, graph, or state and preserves provenance so recomputation cannot resurrect it, and the uncorrected claim is not re-displayed; feeding it back to retrain is the fuller fix (↔ ml-systems [PROV-01], [Principle 12]; ↔ [DATA-14] without a single order authority the copies diverge and the next rebuild restores the error) | Does the underlying record change so recomputation cannot resurrect the error? | B·w | human-in-the-loop-ml |
| HAI-03 | Automation is specified as a single autonomy level (L0–L5, "full auto") traded against the human | **Two-dimensional control × automation** — map each feature on human-control and computer-automation axes and target high control *and* high automation for non-instant loops, or operators lose recovery and performance (↔ biz [STRAT-11] never split the difference into a mush middle) | Is this feature on a 1-D autonomy ladder instead of a control × automation map? | S·p | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-04 | An automated effector or decision path lacks activate, mid-run operate, or override/stop | **Activate–operate–override** — the user can turn it on, steer it while running, and stop it, with intent feedback, or false activations and unrecoverable runs follow (↔ [CAL-02] abstention and defer-to-human are valid results; ↔ [HITL-07] escalate the low-confidence item instead of forcing a machine call; ↔ [RLSE-04] override/stop is a designed state not an afterthought; ↔ [EMB-04] de-emphasise the identity-void sample rather than force a face) | Can the user turn it on, steer it, and stop it? | B·w | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-05 | AI is active with no disclosure, or is named as full autonomy beyond its operating envelope | **Imperceptible AI is not ethical** — disclose that AI is acting and how to disable it, and don't overclaim autonomy beyond the tested envelope, or users over-trust and cannot recover (↔ [CLM-05]; ↔ [ARCH-13] a property left to "everyone will be careful" is perceived not enforced; ↔ [WEB-21] a framed UI steals clicks the eye never chose; ↔ [PG-02] tenant isolation left to every developer's WHERE clause; ↔ [GRPH-18] a similarity chain asserts an entity boundary the data never enforced) | Would an operator know AI is acting and how to disable it? | B·r | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-06 | Production automated decisions have no durable decision / override / failure log | **Audit trail as design fuel** — log inputs, outputs, overrides, and failures so a decision is reconstructable for forensics and refinement, or there is no learning after a near miss (↔ grph [GRPH-14], ml-systems [PROV-01] all require the artifact to point back at what produced it) | Can we reconstruct the last automated decision and its override from logs? | B·w | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-07 | A consequential or life-critical AI ships with recommender-grade process | **Consequence-tiered rigor** — escalate controls, verification, monitoring, and oversight to the outcome's consequence tier, or algorithmic-hubris failures follow | What is this feature's consequence tier, and does the control bar match? | B·p | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-08 | A model claim can change a user's decision but is shown as a bare percentage | **Uncertainty at the decision granularity** — expose the evidence and uncertainty at the granularity the user decides on (frame, track, cluster), not as a decorative confidence number (↔ ml-systems [CAL-02] both make unknown a designed state rather than a forced answer) | Is uncertainty shown where the decision is actually made? | S·w | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-09 | "Trustworthy" or "ethical AI" is asserted with no component reports or independent review | **Trustworthy ≠ trusted** — publish component evidence (data, verification, fairness, explainability, audit, oversight) before a trust claim, or the claim invites mistaken trust (↔ [CLM-05] a trust claim without component evidence is the hostile-reader failure mode) | Which trustworthiness components have open reports with positive results? | S·r | human-centered-ai |
| HAI-10 | Items are shown to human raters in chronological or source order | **Repetition-priming control** — shuffle or diversity-sequence items and audit whether presentation order predicts the label, or order becomes a hidden confound in the training data (↔ ml [FM-03] few-shot example order changes behaviour as materially as code) | Is order randomized, and does it fail to predict the label? | S·w | human-in-the-loop-ml |
| HAI-11 | A complex annotation or review UI offers only accept/reject, with model pre-labels and no edit path | **Interface-mode trade-off** — choose unassisted / assisted / predictive / adjudication with explicit quality and agency costs, and keep an edit path when the model pre-labels, or label quality silently degrades to the model's priors | Can the human correct the model, not just accept it? | B·w | human-in-the-loop-ml |
| HAI-12 | A generative model's output is presented as the finished answer, to be consumed as-is | **Output is a proposal, not the answer** — surface generative output as a designed artifact to interpret, verify, adjust, and act onward from, not as verified truth; for an objective claim that drives a real, high-stakes action, route to a secondary human or external check before presenting it authoritatively (↔ [HAI-07] consequence-tiered rigor, [HAI-08], [INT-11] all treat generated output as revisable rather than final) | Will the user treat this as verified truth, and can a wrong accept cause irreversible harm? | B·w | designing-ai-interfaces |
| HAI-13 | A fluent output can be wrong in a way the system cannot itself flag — no error state, confident hallucination | **User is the last error detector** — when failure is undetectable to the system, make the human the detector by default: afford double-check, inline edit, and source/verification paths, and cue "verify important details" instead of showing a success state (↔ [HAI-01] evidence before label, [HAI-08]) | Is this failure detectable by the system, or only by the user? | B·w | designing-ai-interfaces |
| HAI-14 | On a commercial surface, AI output or an AI agent could reasonably be mistaken for a human or for authoritative human communication | **Disclose the synthetic hand** — label AI-generated or AI-mediated content as an understanding aid when it could pass as human — distinct from disclosing that automation is *acting* (↔ [HAI-05] imperceptible AI; ↔ biz [CLM-05] three hostile readers; ↔ [PERC-03] the surface implies a human group the system does not wire; ↔ [TEAM-01] a boundary that exists only on the diagram) | Could a user reasonably think a human wrote or is speaking this? | S·r | designing-ai-interfaces |
| HAI-15 | The review screen renders the model's decision, its top-ranked candidate, a highlighted match, or an earlier reviewer's conclusion before the reviewer's own judgment is submitted | **Commit before reveal** — when a human is placed in the loop to catch model error, take and store that human's independent judgment before any prior verdict is displayed — the model's label, its top-ranked candidate, or a first reviewer's conclusion — then reveal it for reconciliation, because a displayed prior verdict pulls the human decision toward it whether it is right or wrong and an instruction to ignore the label does not cancel the effect, so a confirm-only screen logs agreement rather than verification (↔ [HAI-11] the interface-mode choice; this row fixes the order inside it; ↔ sec [SECD-04] two authorizers are one authorizer when the second sees the first's answer) | Can the reviewer submit before any prior verdict is on screen? | S·w | forensic-face-matching |
| HAI-16 | A review screen renders a model-ranked candidate list for a forced-choice pick with no cap on the number shown | **Bound the forced-choice set** — when a human must pick the correct entity from a model-ranked shortlist, cap the number rendered at the decision, record that count with the decision, and send the remainder to a second pass, because the chance that some near-miss out-competes the true entity rises with the number of alternatives on screen, and the correct entity is frequently in the list while the human picks a different one (↔ [COG-01] this is a search-and-criterion limit, not a memory-span limit; ↔ ml [RAG-05] the machine-side twin — a ranked list is selected under the consumer's budget, never concatenated whole) | How many candidates were on screen when this decision was recorded? | S·w | forensic-face-matching |
| HAI-17 | A review screen shows one stored reference against the item under review while the store holds several independent captures of the same entity | **Every reference the store holds** — when a human is asked whether two observations are the same entity and the store holds more than one independent capture of the claimed entity, render a reference set spanning the available capture times and conditions instead of a single stored image, because one reference is a single sample of an entity that varies within itself: the true entity is rejected when the lone reference is old or unlike it, and an impostor is accepted when one flattering reference is all there was to compare against (↔ ml [EMB-02] the machine-side twin — one observation must not define the identity; ↔ [HAI-01] the reviewer reaches the decisive evidence in one action) | How many references does the store hold, and how many did the screen render? | S·w | forensic-face-matching |
| HAI-18 | A control that trains, reweights, or changes what the system will do later fires, the screen state is unchanged, and no copy names the future effect | **Name the deferred effect** — when a control's only effect is on future system behaviour, state the direction of that change in the interface at the moment the control fires, because an action with no perceptible result reads as a no-op: the user stops using the control and the correction stream the model learns from dries up at its source (↔ biz [AIPX-03] this control is where the next label comes from; ↔ [PERC-05] post-action feedback the user cannot perceive is feedback that did not happen; ↔ [HAI-02] there is a consequence to state only where the correction actually reaches the source) | After this control fires, what on screen tells the user what will be different next time? | J·w | guidelines-human-ai-interaction |

## 7. VIZ — Data-to-channel encoding, glyph design, colour-for-data, comparison

Governs how evidence is *drawn*: which visual channel carries which data type, how accurately a quantity is read, when a glyph should fuse or separate its variables, how to highlight and layer, and how to lay out comparison. Builds on `PERC` (can the eye see it) rather than restating it; borders `GRPH` (layout/partition algorithms) and `A11Y` (never colour alone). It does **not** own uncertainty display — that is `HAI-08`.

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| VIZ-01 | An ordered attribute is mapped to an identity channel (e.g. unordered hue), or a categorical attribute to a magnitude channel that implies rank | **Expressiveness match** — encode all of and only the attribute's information: ordered data must look ordered (magnitude channels), categorical data must not imply order (identity channels), or the reader sees an order that is not there or misses one that is (Munzner Ch5.4.1) | Do ordered and magnitude attributes look ordered, and do categorical attributes avoid implying a rank? | B·p | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| VIZ-02 | An accuracy-critical quantitative comparison is encoded with area, angle, colour, or texture while position or length is free | **Effectiveness ranking** — give the most accuracy-critical quantity the most accurate free channel: prefer position then length before area, angle, hue, saturation, or density (Ware G5.15 gives the two-class position/length preference; the finer multi-rung ordering is Munzner's ranked synthesis, Fig 5.6 — guidance, not a law) | Is the most accurate free channel carrying the most accuracy-critical quantity? | S·p | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| VIZ-03 | A quantity is mapped to 3D glyph volume, or abstract data is rendered in 3D without a shape-understanding task | **No unjustified 3D** — never map a quantity to volume (viewers judge area and under-read large values) and keep abstract data in 2D; justify 3D only when inherent geometry is the task, because 3D adds occlusion and perspective error (Ware G5.16, Fig 5.26; Munzner Ch6.3) | Is any quantity encoded as 3D volume (never allowed), and separately, is 3D used for non-spatial data with no inherent-shape task? | B·r | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-04 | The stated primary visual query needs two glyph attributes read independently but they sit on an integral pair (e.g. width×height), or needs a ratio/combination read but they sit on separable channels (e.g. colour×size) | **Integral vs separable glyphs** — map a holistic ratio/combination judgment to integral glyph dimensions and independent analytic judgments to separable channels, or the user cannot make the read the task needs (Ware G5.13/G5.14; Munzner Ch5.5.3) | Does glyph integrality match whether the stated query fuses or separates the variables? | S·p | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-05 | Glance-search over many categories requires a conjunction of two features (e.g. colour and shape together) to identify a class | **No conjunction search** — give each searchable class a unique value on one strong channel (colour, motion, orientation, size); if two memberships must be co-shown, split them across motion or space and colour or shape, or search goes serial and targets are missed (Ware G5.11/G5.12; ↔ perc [PERC-06]) | Can each critical class be found by one undoubted feature alone? | S·p | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-06 | A subset is highlighted by dimming everything else, or motion is used while colour and shape are free | **Additive highlight** — mark the target by adding a feature (halo, size-up, underline) on an underused channel rather than stripping features from the rest, and reserve subtle motion for when colour and shape are spent; a dim-the-rest treatment is acceptable only when the target also carries an additive mark (Ware G5.7–G5.9; ↔ perc [PERC-07]) | Is highlighting additive on an underused channel, not subtractive from the field? | S·w | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-07 | A category set or critical structure is carried by hue/colour alone and collapses when desaturated | **Survive greyscale** — encode primary structure and critical categories with a redundant separable channel (shape, position, luminance) so they survive a black-and-white and colour-vision-deficiency check, never colour alone — VIZ owns the identity of data classes in an encoding, a11y owns UI and status tokens (Ware G5.10; Munzner Ch6.9; ↔ a11y [A11Y-06] second channel always; ↔ eng [UI-02] never color alone) | Do categories and the main pattern survive greyscale and CVD simulation? | B·r | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-08 | A continuous rainbow or hue-only ramp encodes a quantitative order or fine spatial structure | **Colormap matches data type** — use a sequential map (monotonic luminance) for an ordered field and diverging only with a true midpoint; avoid the default rainbow for ordered data, which invents order and turns unequal perceived steps into features (Munzner Ch10.3.2, Fig 10.12; the colormap case of [VIZ-01], capacity handled by [VIZ-09]; ↔ a11y) | Does an equal data step look equal, and is there a true midpoint for any diverging map? | B·w | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| VIZ-09 | An attribute's number of levels exceeds the channel's reliably distinguishable steps (line width, small-region hue, saturation, texture density) | **Discriminable bins** — keep an attribute's encoded levels within the channel's discriminable steps at the mark size, else aggregate or switch channel, or readers merge distinct data into fewer perceived levels (Munzner Ch5.5.2, Fig 10.8; Ware G6.16) | How many levels are encoded, and how many bins does this channel afford at this size? | S·w | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| VIZ-10 | Absolute values must be read off a chart that has no reference grid | **Reference lines for absolute reads** — add faint, unobtrusive horizontal/vertical rules that carry the scale into the plot when absolute magnitude (not just ordering) must be read, or only relative comparison is reliable (Ware G5.17, Fig 5.28) | Can absolute values be read without hovering or mental arithmetic? | S·w | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-11 | A channel's semantics conflict with the domain metaphor (temperature as size, amount as hue only, orientation as colour) while a natural channel is free | **Natural channel mapping** — prefer the natural mapping (orientation→orientation, amount→vertical extent, heat→warm–cool) unless an accuracy constraint forces a documented override, or decoding is slow and sometimes inverted (Ware G5.18) | Would a new user decode the channel correctly without the legend? | J·p | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-12 | Semi-transparent or stacked layers where a mark cannot be assigned to its layer, or a dense same-frequency background sits under an overlay | **Layered transparency discipline** — keep layers few (≈2–3) and luminance-distinct, enforce a lawful luminance order, and avoid same-spatial-frequency competition, or the layers fuse and marks are misattributed (Ware Ch6 transparency, Fig 6.47; Munzner Ch12.5) | Can a reviewer assign each mark to its layer without toggling? | B·r | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-13 | A relationship is shown only by colour/position similarity, or a dense directed graph uses tiny arrowheads | **Node-link grammar** — draw entities as small closed shapes and relationships as links, enclosure, or attachment; prefer tapered directed strokes and smooth link paths for tracing, or topology and direction are misread (Ware G6.3/G6.22–G6.24; ↔ grph layout) | Is topology carried by links (not only similarity), and is direction traceable? | S·w | information-visualization-ware |
| VIZ-14 | A detailed comparison across many states is delivered only as multiframe animation the viewer must remember | **Small multiples over animation** — show aligned, same-encoding partitions side by side for detailed multi-state comparison and reserve animation for transitions, or change blindness hides the differences (Munzner Ch6.5/Ch12.3.2; ↔ cog [COG-01]) | Can two non-adjacent states be compared without replaying the animation? | S·p | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| VIZ-15 | Coordinated or overview–detail views do not share an encoding or linked highlighting, or partitions are unaligned | **Coordinate multiple views** — share at least one encoding (especially a linked-highlight colour) and align partitions across related views, so a set selected in one view is traceable in the others (Munzner Ch12.3) | Selecting a set in one view, does the same set light up in the others? | S·w | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| VIZ-16 | The named marks a stated visual query must compare require multi-panel or multi-screen jumps (large saccades at the intended viewing size) | **Compact the visual query** — place the marks one query compares within a compact region so fixations stay short, except in maps or networks where space itself encodes the data, or search turns serial and secondary marks are missed (Ware G5.1, searchlight/UFOV; ↔ perc [PERC-05]) | Are the marks for one comparison within a compact fixation neighbourhood? | S·p | information-visualization-ware |

## 8. UXR — Usability research, metrics, and validation

Governs *what to measure, how to collect it without biasing it, how many participants, and how to report it honestly* — the human-measurement analogue of ml-systems `EVAL`. Fires at **plan** time (pick the metric, design the study) and **review** time (analyse and report). Borders `HAI` (evidence and uncertainty on a decision surface) and ml-systems `EVAL` (match the measure to the claim).

| ID | Trigger | Rule | Answers | T·P | Src |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| UXR-01 | A result is labelled "validated" without naming which nested level it tested, or downstream success is read as proof of an upstream claim | **Match claim to validation level** — bind each claim and its method to the same level of the four (domain / abstraction / idiom / algorithm); a wall-clock timing does not validate an encoding and a lab task does not validate domain fit, and a downstream success never proves an upstream block was right (Munzner Ch4.3/Ch4.6; ↔ ml [EVAL-01]; ↔ [UXR-15] a composite "win" hides a collapsed component) | Which level does this claim target, and does the method test that level? | B·r | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| UXR-02 | An encoding or idiom is evaluated or called "validated" with no written task+data abstraction it serves | **Abstraction before validation** — specify the task and data abstraction (from observing target users at their real work) before evaluating the idiom that serves it, because a study of an encoding whose abstraction is unstated validates nothing (Munzner Ch4.3.1/Ch4.4; ↔ biz [PROD-02] problem before solution) | Is the task+data abstraction written down before we evaluate the encoding that serves it? | S·p | visualization-analysis-design-munzner |
| UXR-03 | A study states a goal (a critical task, problem discovery, a subtle change) but the primary metrics cannot decide that goal | **Choose the metric for the goal** — map the study goal to the metric class that can decide it (critical → binary success plus errors; discovery → issue frequency/severity; subtle live change → A/B or a large online sample), or the study cannot support the decision (Albert & Tullis §3.4; ↔ [UXR-01] downstream success never proves the upstream claim; ↔ [OPS-01] incentives pay for the measured behaviour not the intended one; ↔ [PROD-01] ship what changes valuable behaviour not vanity output; ↔ [AIPX-02] offline score rising while acceptance stays flat; ↔ [OBS-07] page on degraded experience not a CPU threshold; ↔ [TEST-11] coverage is gameable, change-failure is the outcome) | Does each primary metric's class match the goal class it must decide? | B·p | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-04 | Tasks lack a clear end-state, success is scored after the fact, or ordinal levels-of-success are averaged | **Define success before scoring** — predefine the end-state and pass/fail criteria before collecting, use binary when getting close does not count, and show levels-of-success as stacked frequencies rather than a mean (Albert & Tullis §4.1; ↔ [FORE-02] if it cannot resolve it cannot train judgment) | Could two scorers agree on success without reinterpreting the task, and are partial levels shown as a distribution? | B·p | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-05 | A success rate, time, or rating is reported as a bare point with no confidence interval or n | **Report the interval, not the point** — attach n and an uncertainty interval to every headline estimate (Adjusted Wald for a small-sample proportion; an appropriate interval or the distribution for a time or rating), because a bare point from a usability sample is read as population truth (Albert & Tullis §4.1, §1.6 Myth 10; ↔ hai [HAI-08]) | Does every headline estimate show n and an interval whose method matches the statistic? | B·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-06 | A mean time-on-task includes unattended outliers or moderator-stopped failures, or think-aloud runs during a timed path | **Time-on-task hygiene** — state an outlier and give-up rule, analyse successful-task times only when the moderator stops failures, and use retrospective (not concurrent) think-aloud when time is primary, or the efficiency figure is biased (Albert & Tullis §4.2) | Is the time robust to give-ups, outliers, and the verbal protocol? | S·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-07 | Errors are tabulated with no codebook or opportunity denominator, or one event is counted as two error types | **Error rate needs a denominator** — predefine incorrect actions and the opportunity count per task and avoid double-counting, or error rates are incomparable across tasks and inflated (Albert & Tullis §4.3; ↔ ml [EVAL-02] imbalance-aware metrics need the critical-class denominator) | Do error rates share a common definition and a per-task opportunity denominator? | S·p | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-08 | Step/effort efficiency includes early-abandoned tasks, or a navigation study has no path-vs-minimum baseline | **Efficiency on completed work** — compute step efficiency on successful tasks only, use lostness or steps-vs-minimum-path for navigation, and report success-per-unit-time when both speed and completion matter, or early quitters look efficient (Albert & Tullis §4.4; ↔ eng [PERF-03] coordinated omission invents a green average by dropping the hard cases) | Does "efficient" mean less effort on completed work, not a fast give-up? | S·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-09 | A frequent-use product is judged on first exposure yet "learnable" is claimed | **Learnability is measured over trials** — track the performance metric across multiple equated trials and report the learning curve, or a first-use score is mistaken for steady-state proficiency (Albert & Tullis §4.5) | Have we measured change with experience, not only trial one? | S·p | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-10 | Honesty-sensitive ratings are collected in the facilitator's view, or a rating is summarised only by its mean | **Self-report bias and distribution** — collect satisfaction/ease anonymously or out of the facilitator's view and report the rating distribution (frequencies/top-box), not only the mean, or scores inflate and a bimodal split hides (Albert & Tullis §5.1–5.2; ↔ ml [EVAL-04] the aggregate flips the within-slice ranking) | Would users give the same rating unobserved, and does the mean hide a split audience? | S·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-11 | A multi-task study reports only a post-study overall score with no per-task ease | **Post-task ease locates the pain** — collect a per-task ease rating (e.g. SEQ) after each task to rank tasks for fix priority, or a global score buries the one broken task (Albert & Tullis §5.3; ↔ ml [EVAL-04] slice-based gate — the aggregate buries the broken slice) | Which task should we fix first according to the users who did it? | S·p | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-12 | SUS is reported as raw item averages, with reverse items unflipped, or interpreted by an invented cutoff instead of the published bands | **Score and interpret SUS correctly** — transform to 0–100 with correct reverse coding and interpret against the published medians (~69/~71) and acceptability bands rather than an invented single cutoff; report n and an interval via [UXR-05] (Albert & Tullis §5.4.1) | Is SUS computed and interpreted the way the instrument requires? | B·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-13 | Usability issues are listed with no severity rubric, or one rater's severity is treated as ground truth | **Severity needs a shared rubric** — rate issue severity with a published impact rubric (failure-linked high/medium/low), have at least two people rate independently and reconcile, and treat severity as useful but imperfect (the evaluator effect), or cosmetics get fixed while a failure path is ignored (Albert & Tullis §6.3; ↔ ml [HITL-05] agreement is a diagnostic, not the truth) | Why is this issue high severity, and would a second rater agree? | S·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-14 | A report claims complete or exhaustive issue discovery from a small sample (e.g. ~5 participants), or refuses all inference because n<30 | **Match participant count to the goal** — tie n to the goal class (5–10 may suffice for simple iterative discovery that can tolerate misses; scale up with evaluators for critical, diverse, or statistical goals), and never claim exhaustive coverage from a small n (Albert & Tullis §6.7; ↔ ml [MLDATA-01] reject bare nonprobability sampling for coverage claims) | What residual risk of missed issues does this participant count accept? | B·p | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |
| UXR-15 | A decision deck leads with one composite UX score and no component metrics or weights | **Show the composite's components** — document a combined UX score's components, scales, and weights and always show the per-metric breakdown beside it, or a weighted "win" hides a collapse in a critical dimension (Albert & Tullis §3.4.2; ↔ hai [HAI-08]; ↔ [UXR-03] the metric must be able to decide the goal; ↔ [AIPX-04] aggregate accuracy clearing while a slice fails; ↔ [SEC-12] clean accuracy is the number paid for, not the backdoor) | Can we see which dimension drives the combined score? | S·r | measuring-the-ux-albert-tullis |

## 9. Cross-lexicon links

Interaction & UX borders the visual, implementation, accessibility, and model layers it sits between:

- **Visual tokens (engineering `UI-*`, design `LAY/COL`).** `UI-*` owns the metric — spacing scale, contrast ratio, type ramp; PERC owns the **meaning** those metrics carry. `PERC-01` (proximity) and `INT-03` (button grouping) are the perceptual reason behind spacing and grouping tokens; a value-first palette `[COL-04]` is what lets figure-ground and pop survive greyscale.
- **Accessibility is a hard constraint on every row.** Visual hierarchy must also be programmatic (`PERC-01`/`PERC-03`), pop and status must not be colour-only (`PERC-05`/`PERC-06`), item tools and forms must be operable without a mouse and with real labels (`INT-04`, `FORM-03`, `FORM-06`). Where a UX rule and an `A11Y` rule fire together, the accessibility one is the floor, not a trade.
- **Grouping is one audit in two lanes (↔ `GRPH`).** `PERC-03` (Gestalt audit) and `NAV-05` (MECE) are `[GRPH-22]` (partition-plus-verification) in perceptual and IA form — propose a grouping, then verify it invents no false relation. A dual-home nav label fails the same audit as a dual-cluster membership.
- **Correction, preview, and undo meet ml-systems.** `HAI-02` (correction reaches the source), `INT-09` (reversible command stack), and `INT-07` (preview before commit) are the human-surface form of the model-side obligations in `[PROV-01]` and `[CAL-02]`; the families meet on the review surface where a person acts on a model claim. `HAI-01`/`HAI-06` are `[PROV-01]` and `[Principle 9]` seen from the UI.
- **Memory and consent are security surfaces (↔ `SEC`).** `COG-02` (recognition over recall) is why unusable authentication fails as a control — an overloaded recall burden becomes written-down or weak credentials; `FORM-04` (no dark defaults) keeps a consent boundary from being crossed by an unchosen default.
- **Claims discipline.** `HAI-05` (imperceptible AI) and `HAI-09` (trustworthy ≠ trusted) are `[CLM-05]` (a claim needs inspectable evidence) applied to automation.
- **Encoding builds on perception and borders graph and accessibility (↔ `PERC`, `GRPH`, `A11Y`).** `VIZ-*` is the layer above `PERC`: PERC asks *can the eye see it*, VIZ asks *does the encoding match the data and the query*. `VIZ-05` extends `PERC-06` (single-target pop) to multi-class search; `VIZ-16` extends `PERC-05` (fovea) to query layout; `VIZ-07` (survive greyscale) is the `A11Y` non-colour floor from the data side; `VIZ-13` (node-link grammar) draws what `GRPH-*` lays out. `VIZ` does not own uncertainty display — `HAI-08` does.
- **Measurement is the human twin of model evaluation (↔ ml-systems `EVAL`, `HAI`).** `UXR-01`/`UXR-03` (validate at the level of the claim; measure the goal, not a proxy) are `[EVAL-01]` and `[Principle 4]` for human studies; `UXR-05`/`UXR-15` (report the interval; show the composite's components) are `[HAI-08]` (uncertainty at the decision granularity) seen from the research side. Where a UX number will drive a decision, `UXR` governs whether it was valid to report; the writing lexicon governs how it is phrased.

## 10. Consumption

Referenced by ID at the decision point, never read front-to-back. `PERC-*` fire in a **review** pass over a screenshot — greyscale, blur, and unlabeled-grouping inspection catch most violations; `COG-*` and `NAV-*` fire at **plan** time on flows, IA, and forms; `INT-*`/`FORM-*` at **write** time on actions and inputs; `HAI-*` on any surface where a human acts on a model output; `VIZ-*` fire on a **chart or encoding** — greyscale, data-to-channel mapping, and a "which query does this serve" check catch most violations; `UXR-*` fire at **plan** time on a study design and metric choice and at **review** time on a results table or report. A CSS or component diff routes to `PERC + UI + A11Y`; a form to `FORM + A11Y`; a chart or timeline to `VIZ + PERC + A11Y`; a cluster-review screen to `VIZ + PERC + HAI + GRPH + ml-systems`; a usability study or metrics deck to `UXR + HAI + ml-systems [EVAL]`. A consuming harness inlines the trigger plus the question by phase tag and cites the ID; the mechanism stays in `distilled/interaction/`.
